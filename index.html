02

Fix these first

These are the ones I'd start with. A couple of them are costing you calls this week.

The footer phone number dials the wrong number Losing calls

The phone number down in the footer shows the right number (812-376-7200), but the link hidden behind it is set to a fake placeholder number the website template came with. So it looks correct, but tapping it calls a dead number.

How to check it yourself: on your phone, press and hold the phone number in the footer. The little preview that pops up will show a "123-456-7890" number, not yours. The number at the very top of the page works, but only because phones are smart enough to guess it, not because the site is set up right.
The fix: Point every phone number on the site at the real one so it dials the shop every time, top and bottom of the page.

You can't see what you type in the contact form Losing leads

On the Contact page, when someone types their name, email, or message, the text comes out white on a white box, so it's basically invisible. You can't read what you're entering or check it for typos. A lot of people will assume the form is broken and give up right there. (The homepage form has a smaller version of this: the labels vanish the second you start typing.)

The fix: Make the typed text a normal dark color so people can see and double-check what they wrote, and keep a label showing above every box.

Clicking the logo doesn't take people home Navigation

On every page, the logo up top is linked to the page you're already on instead of the homepage. So if someone's deep in a product page and clicks the logo to get back to the start, it just reloads the same page. People expect a logo to take them home, and when it doesn't, they feel stuck and leave.

The fix: Set the logo to link back to the homepage across the whole site.

Nothing is counting who visits your site No data

There's no visitor tracking on the site at all. That means there's no way to know how many people come by, what they click on, whether anyone uses the contact form, or what they typed into Google to find you.

Why it matters: without this, every decision about the site is a guess. With it, you can see what's actually working and put your effort where it pays off.
The fix: Turn on Google Analytics and Google Search Console, both free. Search Console alone will show you the exact searches bringing people to your site.

Google is writing your search listing for you Google

When your shop shows up in a Google search, there's a headline and a short gray summary line under it. Right now yours are blank or generic, so Google grabs whatever text it wants to fill them in. You're letting Google decide how you show up instead of writing it yourself.

Good news: the tool that controls this is already installed and paid for. Nobody ever filled in the boxes. This is filling in blanks, not building anything new.
The fix: Write a real headline and summary for each page. For the homepage, something like: "Outdoor Power Equipment, Parts & Small Engine Repair in Columbus, IN."

Texting your website shows a map pin, not your logo First impression

Send your website link to someone in a text. Instead of your logo or a photo of the shop, it pops up as a plain gray location pin. That's the very first thing a new customer sees, before they even open the site, and it reads as unfinished. It happens because the site was never given a proper preview image, so it falls back to a generic pin.

The fix: Set a preview image, your logo or a clean storefront photo, so every link you share shows the business instead of a blank pin.

Two reviews name a company customers won't recognize Trust

Two of the three customer reviews on the homepage mention "Grass Luvers." If that's the shop's old name, those reviews are real and worth keeping, but a new customer who only knows "Columbus Outdoor Power" hits a name they've never heard of halfway through, and it reads like it was copied from somewhere else.

The fix: Either update the reviews to say Columbus Outdoor Power, or add a small "formerly Grass Luvers" line so the name connects. With 56 Google reviews, there are plenty of fresh ones to pull from.
03

Getting found on Google

For a local shop, showing up when someone searches "lawn mower repair near me" or "Scag dealer Columbus" matters more than anything else here. This is where you've got the most room to grow.

Google can't see your address, hours, or star rating Local search

Behind the scenes, there's a spot where the website hands Google the basic facts about your business, name, address, phone, hours, star rating. Right now yours is nearly empty. That's the main reason your 4.3 stars from 56 reviews never show up as gold stars next to your name in search results: Google simply can't find them.

The fix: Fill in the business details and your rating so Google can show your stars. This is one of the highest-payoff things on the whole list.

Make sure your Google Business Profile is fully built out Off the website

This is the map listing that shows up above the regular Google results. For a shop like yours, it drives more phone calls than the website itself. It's separate from the site, but worth doing.

  • Confirm it's claimed, with current hours and 15 to 20 real photos
  • Reply to every review, good and bad, so it looks active
  • Ask every happy customer to leave one. More recent reviews push you higher

Build a page for each brand you carry Content

People search by brand, "Husqvarna dealer near me," "Echo dealer Columbus." Right now the site only has one brand set up (Scag), so you show up for none of the others even though you sell them.

The fix: A simple page for each brand you carry, listing what you stock and that you service it.

Get listed in the manufacturer "find a dealer" tools Free traffic

Scag, Husqvarna, Echo, and the rest each have a "find a dealer near you" tool on their own website. Those send ready-to-buy people straight to authorized dealers, and being listed is usually free and comes with a link back to your site that Google trusts. Worth making sure you're on every one, for every brand you carry, with the right address and a working link.

The fix: Claim or update your dealer listing on each manufacturer's site. Free referral traffic, plus a credibility boost with Google.

Show up the same everywhere people look Local listings

People find local shops through more than Google: Apple Maps, Bing, Yelp, Facebook. Your name, address, and phone need to match exactly across all of them. When they don't match, Google trusts you a little less and you slip in the local results. Quiet, unglamorous work that genuinely moves the needle.

The fix: Get listed or corrected on Apple Maps, Bing, Yelp, and Facebook, with identical name, address, and phone everywhere.

A couple of small things to tidy up Housekeeping

Two small ones. The blog only has the default "Hello world!" test post from years back, and a few leftover setup pages are still public. Neither helps you, and an empty blog can even tell Google the site is inactive. Down the road, simple posts like "when to sharpen your mower blades" are exactly what pulls in local searches.

The fix: Delete the test post and the leftover pages. Either keep the blog going with a few useful seasonal posts a year, or hide it for now.

Can you outrank Lowe's and Menards? The honest answer The strategy

On broad searches like "lawn mower," no, and it's not worth chasing. Those giant sites win the generic terms every time. But that's not where your customers are. You win the searches they can't: "small engine repair near me," "Scag dealer Columbus," "Husqvarna blade for [model]." Big box stores don't repair equipment, aren't authorized dealers, and don't have pages for specific parts. And in the local map box up top, you can sit right at or above them, because that box rewards being close and relevant, not being a huge company.

The play: Don't fight the big stores where they're strong. Own the local, service, dealer, and specific-part searches where you're genuinely the better answer, and where the people who'll actually drive to your shop are looking.
04

Your products & selling online

The website shows only a tiny slice of what you actually sell. There's a real opportunity to fix that.

Your site shows 12 mowers, but you sell so much more Opportunity

Right now it's twelve big Scag zero-turns (priced, with a "Call to Order" button), one category, one brand. The things you move every day, oils, filters, blades, trimmer line, chainsaw bars, replacement parts, aren't on the site yet, and neither are your other brands. Someone landing here would think you sell a dozen products, when you actually carry a whole store's worth.

The fix: Get the real range online. Even if the small parts aren't for sale online (more on that below), just having them listed and searchable makes you look like the full-line dealer you are, and every one is another way to get found on Google.

Organize it so people can actually browse Structure

Right now it's one flat list. With your number of items, you want real sections so a customer (and Google) can find things:

  • By equipment type: mowers, trimmers, chainsaws, blowers, snow, generators
  • By brand: let people filter to Husqvarna, Scag, Echo, and so on
  • Parts & supplies: oils, filters, blades, belts, trimmer line, grouped together
  • A working search box, and a price or a clear "call for pricing" on every item

Add a short form next to the call button Capturing leads

Right now each product's only button is "Call to Order," and it just drops people on your general contact page. The bigger issue: most people browsing at night or on a lunch break won't call. If a phone call is the only option, they leave and you never hear from them.

Why a form matters: a short form ("name, email, which machine you're interested in") captures that person so you can follow up on your own time, and you keep their email. A phone call gives you one conversation; a saved email lets you reach back out. Keep the call button for the ready-to-buy folks, and add the form for everyone else.
The fix: Put a working call button and a short "request info" form on every product page.

How selling parts online would actually work The plan

The right home for the small stuff (oils, filters, blades, trimmer line) is Shopify. You load your products once, and it runs the store, the payments, and the checkout for you. The best part for you: it's easy to manage yourself. You add and price items in a few clicks, no code and no developer needed for everyday changes.

To answer what you asked directly: a customer buys online, then picks how they get it. Both options sit right there in the checkout.

Customer picks at checkout

Pick up at the shop

They pay online and swing by to grab it. No shipping cost, and it gets them through your door, where regulars tend to grab something else too.

Customer picks at checkout

Ship it to them

For anyone not local, they cover shipping and it goes out in the mail. You set the rates. This opens you up to customers well outside Columbus.

The big equipment stays exactly as it is: shown online, sold in person. No card fees on a $14,000 machine, and nobody's shipping a mower. Only the small, easy-to-handle items get a buy button.
05

Turning contacts into customers

You're collecting contact info but not doing anything with it yet. This is money left on the table.

Where do your form messages actually land? Worth checking

First thing to confirm: when someone fills out your contact form, where does that message go? Make sure it's landing in an inbox you check every day, not a spam folder or an old email address the developer plugged in and forgot about.

The fix: Fill out your own form as a test and confirm the message actually arrives where you'll see it. Simple, but easy to overlook, and a broken form silently swallows every lead.

You can collect emails, but you can't market to them yet Missed revenue

Right now your form only asks for a name, phone, email, and message. It's built to answer one question, not to build a customer list. Two pieces are missing: a checkbox where people agree to hear from you, and an email tool to store and send to them.

Why it matters: without that checkbox, you're not supposed to email those contacts marketing later, even for a sale or an event. Most email tools won't let you, and blasting people who only asked a service question is a fast way to land in the spam folder. So today, when you run a big event or a seasonal sale, you have no way to reach the people who already raised their hand.
The fix: Add a simple opt-in checkbox ("Send me deals, events, and seasonal reminders") and connect a free email tool like Mailchimp. Then every person who contacts you can become someone you're allowed to email, for a sale, an event, or a "time to winterize" reminder. For a local shop, that list is one of the most valuable things you can build.

Look & feel

The design is one of the stronger parts. These are small changes that help turn a visitor into a phone call. Checked on both desktop and phone.

Make the buttons obvious where people decide to act Getting calls

A few spots could point people to the next step more clearly:

  • The very top of the homepage has a headline and a paragraph but no button. Add one clear action there: "Shop Equipment," "Call the Shop," or "Get Directions."
  • At the bottom, "Contact Us Today" shows up as plain text instead of a button, so it blends in. Make it a solid orange button like the "More About Us" one you already have.
  • After someone sends the contact form, show a clear "thanks, we got it" message so they know it worked.

What's genuinely good, leave it alone

  • Clean, consistent orange-and-black branding that suits the industry
  • Real photos of your showroom and equipment, not generic stock images
  • The "Our Services" and "Built on Reliable Service" sections are strong
  • It works properly on a phone, nothing broken in the mobile layout
  • Solid, professional software underneath, with the right tools already installed
  • Google map and clear hours and address in the footer
07

Speed

Not broken, just heavier than it needs to be, which slows the page and can nudge you down in Google.

The page loads a lot of extra weight Performance

The site is built with a drag-and-drop page builder that loads a big pile of files every time a page opens. It's a common trade-off, easy to edit, but heavier to load. Some basic tuning would make it noticeably snappier.

The fix: Add a speed plugin, convert the photos to a lighter format, and run the site through Google's free "PageSpeed Insights" tool for a specific to-do list.
